Document 01 · Product Overview

SketchForge.
Draw it. We build it.

A mobile-first creative tool that turns hand sketches of physical objects — pipes, structures, assemblies, furniture — into accurate, photorealistic representations through conversational refinement.

StatusConcept · Pre-MVP
PlatformiOS · Android (Flutter)
Primary UsersDesigners · Engineers · Hobbyists
DomainConstruction · Mechanical · Furniture

§ 01The premise

The gap between an idea and a usable visual representation is currently filled by either expensive software (CAD), expensive talent (designers), or unreliable AI (text-to-image). SketchForge proposes a third path: the user draws what they already see in their head — rough, approximate, mobile — and the system collaborates with them to produce something accurate.

The product is not a magic black box. It is a conversation between a person who knows what they want and a system that knows how to render it correctly. The sketch is the starting prompt, not the final input.

§ 02The four pillars

PILLAR 01

Sketch fidelity

Every stroke, in order, with pressure and timing — captured as structured data, not just pixels. The drawing itself becomes a parse tree.

PILLAR 02

Semantic understanding

Vision models interpret the sketch against a closed vocabulary of construction primitives — cylinders, pipes, plates, joints — rather than guessing freely.

PILLAR 03

Conversational refinement

The system asks the right questions: material, dimensions, context, style. The user's answers shape the output until it matches their intent.

PILLAR 04

Verified rendering

Outputs come from two paths: procedural 3D rendering for geometric exactness, or AI generation with structural locks for photoreal style.

§ 03Feature inventory

The following are the features that comprise the v1 product. Each is presented with what it is, and why it is — because features without intent become bloat.

F-001

Precision sketch canvas

A drawing surface that captures every stroke as vector data — coordinates, timing, pressure where available, and stroke order. Users draw with finger or stylus on a subtle grid background that suggests scale without imposing it.

A bitmap loses the user's reasoning. A vector record preserves it. When the user draws the outer cylinder first and then the inserted pipe, that order is signal — it tells the interpreter what is structure and what is detail.

F-002

Domain selection

Before drawing, the user selects a domain — mechanical, plumbing, structural, or furniture. This biases the interpretation engine toward sensible readings within that context.

The same sketch of a vertical cylinder with a horizontal line through it means different things in different contexts: a pump in mechanical, a tee fitting in plumbing, a column with a beam in structural, a table leg with a stretcher in furniture. Domain context collapses ambiguity before it costs the user time.

F-003

Voice annotation while drawing

Users can hold a microphone button and narrate as they draw — "this is a 1.5 meter steel cylinder, the line through it is a 50mm copper pipe." Speech is transcribed on-device and bound to the active stroke.

Visual interpretation alone caps out at roughly 70-85% accuracy on free sketches. Voice adds a redundant channel — geometry plus intent plus material — that cross-validates the interpretation and lifts effective accuracy near 99%.

F-004

Geometric primitive detection

On submission, an on-device pass identifies geometric primitives — rectangles, circles, ellipses, straight lines, arcs — and their spatial relationships. The result is a structured description: "rectangle at top, two circles inside it, horizontal line crossing the lower third."

Vision-language models are unreliable narrators of geometry. They name objects well and measure them poorly. By extracting the geometry deterministically first, the interpretation step only has to handle the semantic question — what does this configuration mean? — not the perceptual one.

F-005

Semantic interpretation

A multimodal AI model receives the sketch image, the structured primitives, the domain tag, and any voice annotation. It returns a structured assembly specification: an ordered list of named parts, their dimensions, and their relationships.

A free-text description is unverifiable. A structured spec — "cylinder(h=1.5m, d=0.3m, material=steel) + pipe(d=0.05m, length=0.8m, material=copper, relation=horizontal_through_midpoint)" — can be rendered, validated, and corrected. Structure is what makes precision possible.

F-006

Validation overlay

Before generating any final image, the system renders a clean schematic interpretation of what it understood — perfect circles, straight lines, labeled parts — overlaid semi-transparent on the user's sketch. The user can tap any element to correct it.

This is the single largest precision multiplier in the system. Raw AI interpretation accuracy of 80% becomes effective accuracy of 99% the moment a human confirms or corrects. It also turns errors into a teaching moment rather than a frustration.

F-007

Conversational refinement

Once the interpretation is confirmed, the system asks contextual follow-up questions tailored to the object. For a cylinder-with-pipe assembly: material, dimensions, context (industrial / residential / lab), render style (photoreal / blueprint / CAD). Each answer is a tappable option, not a typed field.

Mobile typing is friction. Tappable choices feel conversational rather than transactional. And constraining the user to curated options means every answer maps cleanly to the generation prompt — no parsing, no ambiguity.

F-008

Dual-path rendering

The system offers two output modes from the same validated spec: a procedural 3D render built from exact parametric primitives, or a photorealistic AI render conditioned on the original sketch for structural fidelity. Users can flip between them.

Procedural is geometrically perfect — a cylinder is exactly a cylinder, at exactly the specified dimensions. AI is aesthetically richer — surface texture, lighting, environment. Different jobs need different outputs. Offering both means the user never has to choose between accuracy and beauty.

F-009

Iterative refinement loop

After the first render, users can request adjustments in natural language: "make the pipe thicker," "change to brushed steel," "industrial workshop background instead." The system updates the spec and re-renders without losing the original geometric lock.

Real design is iterative. First-shot perfection is a fantasy. Letting users converge on the right output through small adjustments is more honest than promising magic.

F-010

Project history

Every sketch, interpretation, refinement, and render is saved as a versioned project. Users can revisit, branch, or export any state at any time.

A sketch app that forgets is a sketch app that loses trust. History is also the data foundation for future model fine-tuning — the corpus of "what users actually meant" versus "what the system first guessed."

F-011

Export & share

Final outputs export as PNG (raster), SVG (vector schematic), GLB (3D model for procedural path), or a structured JSON spec for downstream tools. Sharing produces a deep link that opens the project in another user's app or browser.

SketchForge is rarely the final destination. A plumber sketches a fitting, exports the schematic, sends it to a supplier. A designer drafts a chair, exports the GLB, drops it into a visualization. Easy export means the tool fits real workflows.

§ 04Domain anchoring

SketchForge ships with four initial domains. Each is a closed vocabulary of primitives and relations the system recognizes natively. New domains can be added without retraining the core models.

DOMAIN 01
Mechanical

Cylinders, shafts, gears, flanges, bearings, brackets, mounting plates. Example: a hydraulic ram with cap and rod end, rendered as a polished steel component.

DOMAIN 02
Plumbing

Pipes, elbows, tees, valves, tanks, junctions, reducers. Example: a vertical storage tank with inlet, outlet, and pressure relief, rendered in copper or PVC.

DOMAIN 03
Structural

Beams, columns, plates, trusses, connections, footings. Example: an I-beam supported by two columns with bolted plate connections, rendered as construction-stage steel.

DOMAIN 04
Furniture

Legs, panels, tops, shelves, joints, fasteners. Example: a four-leg table with stretchers and a rectangular top, rendered in oak or walnut.

§ 06Product principles

Five non-negotiables that govern feature decisions.

PRINCIPLE 01
The user is the author, not the prompt.

The sketch is theirs. The interpretation is a hypothesis they confirm or correct. The system never overrides the user's intent in pursuit of a "better" image.

PRINCIPLE 02
Precision over impression.

A geometrically correct render of a slightly wrong interpretation is worse than an honest schematic. We surface our confidence and let users correct us early.

PRINCIPLE 03
Conversation over command.

No long forms, no jargon-heavy menus. Tap, draw, speak, confirm. Every interaction is one of these four.

PRINCIPLE 04
Mobile is the home, not the port.

The product is designed for thumbs and styluses on a phone. Desktop, when it exists, is the secondary surface — not the other way around.

PRINCIPLE 05
Honest about what AI can do.

We do not promise 99.9% accuracy from raw sketches because the field cannot deliver it. We promise 99% effective accuracy through human-in-the-loop confirmation, and we say so.

On the question of "99.9% precision"

End-to-end sketch interpretation by vision-language models currently caps at roughly 70-85% accuracy on free-form drawings — confirmed by published benchmarks across MeasureBench, Sketch2Code, and SketchJudge. SketchForge does not pretend otherwise.

What we deliver is effective precision near 99% through a three-part strategy: a closed vocabulary that constrains the problem, a validation overlay that catches errors before they cost the user time, and a procedural rendering path that produces geometrically exact output once the spec is confirmed.

The 99.9% number is achievable in one place: the procedural render itself, which is mathematically deterministic. Everywhere else, we are honest about the tradeoff.

§ 07What we are not building (yet)

  • Free-form artistic sketching. SketchForge is for objects, not illustrations. Portraits, landscapes, and creative drawings are out of scope.
  • Full CAD export. v1 exports a structured spec and a GLB. Full STEP / IGES export with constraints comes later, if at all.
  • Multi-user collaboration. Sharing is one-way. Real-time co-editing is v3 territory.
  • AR placement. Dropping the rendered object into a camera view of the user's room is on the roadmap, not in v1.
  • Custom domain creation. Users get our four domains. The ability to define their own primitive vocabulary is enterprise / pro tier later.

MVP

Single domain (mechanical). Canvas, primitive detection, AI interpretation, validation overlay, AI render only. ~6 weeks.

v1.0

All four domains, voice annotation, procedural render path, iterative refinement, project history, export. ~3 months from MVP.

v1.5

Web companion, deep-link sharing, GLB export. Pro tier with higher generation quotas and faster generation models. ~6 months from MVP.
